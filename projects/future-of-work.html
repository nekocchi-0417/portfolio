Designing the Future of Work in a Post-pandemic world

A foresight-driven, conceptual design project for Johnson's Control Innovation Challenge.

Overview

This project took place during the height of the COVID-19 pandemic, as part of an innovation challenge in collaboration with Johnson Controls — a global leader in smart building solutions and workplace technologies.

The challenge was closely aligned with their core business: reimagining the future of workspace. They were particularly interested in anticipating how the needs, expectations, and behaviours of the future workforce might shift, and how their workplace design and technology solutions would need to evolve in response.

Problem

The pandemic reshaped employee expectations, particularly among Gen Y and Z, leading to a talent retention crisis marked by the Great Resignation. Traditional workspace and technology designs face an impending need to evolve alongside the shifting needs of a workforce seeking flexibility, autonomy, and purpose.

Solution

We proposed designing for flexibility through:

  • Reconfigurable workplace designs to support diverse working styles and needs.
  • New scheduling tools to accommodate different work rhythms.
  • Internal talent mobility platforms to enable growth opportunities.

1. User Research and Problem Definition

In 2021, amidst the pandemic, a pervasive workplace phenomenon emerged — a talent retention crisis termed as "The Great Resignation". The pandemic had not only redefined ways of working but also prompted employees to rethink what they are seeking in their careers. More alarmingly, there is a clear generational divide in the job aspirations and behavioural patterns of Gen Y and Z, compared to Gen X.

We conducted site visits to ~4 workplaces and interviewed ~10 employees to understand their expectations and experiences in the workplace since the onset of pandemic. Participants were selected to ensure diverse representation across roles, sectors, and demographics.

We identified 3 fundamental tensions in the emerging work behaviours and expectations of employees:

Finding 1

There is a strong preference for flexible work hours. Yet, some dislike the blurring of work-life boundaries.

"I prefer to choose my own working hours so I can work during my peak productivity periods." vs. "It's honestly much harder to know when to stop working when there's no such thing as 'fixed working hours', and messaging after work becomes more common."

Finding 2

Remote working increases individual productivity but erodes collaboration and social capital.

"I can clear so much more tasks now in a day, without having to travel and being distracted by my co-workers." vs. "Something feels off when there's no face-to-face, you know. You feel less connected and engaged."

Finding 3

Learning and career growth is valued, but competing work priorities leave little time for upskilling.

"Upskilling is one of my top priority when it comes to my career, especially with the pandemic and how fast the economy is changing." vs. "We do have courses and workshops, but honestly where got time..."

Surveys

We also administered an online Qualtrics survey to ~70 participants across different generations (Gen X, Y, and Z) to better understand how career priorities differ across generations and how perceptions of workplace productivity, culture, and career growth opportunities correlate with employee wellbeing.

Across our findings, flexibility emerged as a critical enabler of employee wellbeing, productivity, and retention. We scanned ~20 consultancy reports related to the Future of Work to identify emerging trends, signals, and risks/opportunities.

2. Ideation and Prototyping

We then went through intensive rounds of ideation, affinity mapping, and prioritisation. Given the conceptual and blue-sky nature of the brief, we explored a wide breadth of ideas — pushing beyond the boundaries of today's workplace design to surface bold, future-oriented possibilities.

We ideated extensively using methods like Crazy Eights (8 ideas in 1 min), Brainwriting (sketching ideas and building on each others' in a round robin manner), and Reverse Brainstorming (flip assumptions — e.g., "What if offices NEVER had desks"). Then we did affinity mapping where we clustered ideas by themes.

We eventually landed on 3 key solutions:

  1. Reconfigurable workplace designs to support productive working AND collaborative meetings.
  2. Flexible scheduling tool to facilitate better work-life integration by each user's definition.
  3. Internal talent mobility platforms to enable growth opportunities while promoting retention.

We mainly prototyped the physical workspace design of a "flexipod" — which enables a protected space for individual deep work, but can also be reconfigured into a collaborative space for team meetings and hybrid calls.

3. Presentation

The final design solutions were pitched to the senior management of Johnson Controls' Innovation department.

And alas — we won first runner-up! 🎉
