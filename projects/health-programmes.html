Designing for sustained engagement in national health programmes

A service design project to co-create improvements for a national-level preventive health programme with various healthcare partners.

Main officer driving the project, including scoping the brief and workshops, coordinating vendors and stakeholders, and synthesising workshop output

Team of 3 officers (including a mid- and senior-level officer)
1–2 consultants supporting workshop scoping and facilitation

6 months

1Securing Buy-in 2Ecosystem mapping & Insights Gathering 3Problem definition & Prioritisation 4Co-creation & Solutioning

Problem

In response to Singapore's aging population and the rising prevalence of chronic conditions, the government has shifted its focus from curative care to preventive health. As part of this national pivot toward healthier lifestyles, a national-level preventive health programme was rolled out.

While initial programme enrolment rates was high, engagement waned significantly over time, hindering the formation of sustained behavioural change necessary for long-term health outcomes.

There was a lack of consensus over why this drop-off occurred and how to address it systemically. The experience was fragmented across digital, physical, and relational touchpoints, with no shared blueprint for continuity.

Solution

Through a series of co-creation workshops with partners across policy, ops, healthcare and community service providers, we aligned on pain points, surfaced shared priorities, and brainstormed on possible enhancements. These included workflow/protocol redesigns, role realignments, and behavioural nudges anchored in resident needs.

The process culminated in a future-state service journey, which is now being used to inform implementation planning and system coordination efforts.

1. Project Genesis

This project started with a shared interest between my team lead and I in strengthening the longer-term engagement of our preventive health programme. I began with synthesising feedback on key barriers and needs from user studies, engaging operations teams (including call centers) to understand constraints on the ground, and framing the issue as a design opportunity.

We subsequently pitched the case for a co-creation initiative to senior management, positioning it as a way to align fragmented efforts and drive longer-term outcomes.

This helped secure buy-in to launch a 6-month co-creation workstream involving over 20 stakeholders across 10 teams from 6 different agencies and clusters — spanning policy, operations, and frontline healthcare providers.

2. Ecosystem mapping & Insights gathering

Based on user studies and operational data, we had an initial sensing of the issue.

There is an experience and service gap: On one hand, citizens expressed frustrations over not having appropriate resources and support to help them take actions in their health journey. On the other hand, there are numerous programmes and services available, with very low adoption rate. These often existed in silos.

Therefore, in the kick-off workshop to this co-creation workstream, we started with:

  • Mapping the landscape of available programmes and services, including their target segments, touchpoints, service pathways, and target behaviours.
  • Stakeholders' challenges in driving uptake and sustained engagement in respective programmes/services.

This surfaced key structural gaps (e.g., policy clarity on roles and resource constraints), as well as opportunities for improved integration/coordination of service pathways.

3. Problem definition and prioritisation

Building on the shared understanding from the kick-off workshop, we worked closely with the 20+ stakeholders to identify key resident segments and the programmes and services most critical/promising to sustaining engagement.

More than 150 problem statements were surfaced, which we clustered and synthesised — ultimately narrowing down to 6 priority problem areas that presented the highest potential for impact and feasibility.

A voting exercise was conducted to further refine and align on the top 2–3 common focus areas, including the target segment and service pathway.

Solutioning for other focus areas specific to stakeholders' expertise was discussed separately via one-on-one stakeholder engagement sessions.

This exercise helped generate clarity over both the shared challenges and opportunities for collaboration across agencies, and laid the groundwork for targeted solutioning in subsequent workshops.

4. Co-creation and solutioning

We then mapped the current-state service journey for the prioritised segment/programme based on existing operational protocols.

This draft was validated with frontline service providers, who revealed significant discrepancies between protocol and practice — many of the intended steps were not implemented on the ground, resulting in a truncated and inconsistent service experience.

Building on this, stakeholders ideated and prioritised potential solutions. The ideation was facilitated through a framework of up-, mid-, and down-stream solutions, to encourage stakeholders to brainstorm across the spectrum of interventions:

  • Upstream solutions focused on systemic levers like policy changes and system integration.
  • Midstream solutions targeted programme and operational enhancements, like workflow redesigns.
  • Downstream solutions involved direct resident-facing interventions like communication improvements, and nudges/messages.

We then synthesised the prioritised interventions to develop an ideal-state service journey. This future-state service journey map was brought back to stakeholders for validation. During which new ideas were surfaced — particularly around how adjacent service touchpoints could be enhanced and better integrated to support sustained engagement.

The final set of recommendations was presented to senior management. These outputs are used to inform the development of a strategic roadmap for the programme — including the design of supporting operational processes and technological enablers.

Learnings

  1. Feedback loops (especially frontline validation) are critical. Protocols and guidelines often overlook operational constraints. For solutions to be effective, they must be grounded in the operational realities and shaped by those delivering the service on the ground.
  2. Shared artifacts drive clarity and alignment. Visualising solutions through tools like a service map helps to clarify how the interventions implicate or improve the end-to-end experience. These artifacts also serve as neutral reference points for diverse teams to align on priorities, surface gaps/dependencies, and brainstorm solutions more effectively.
  3. Balance in-depth upstream problem identification with tangible downstream impact. While long-term structural improvements (e.g., policy or system redesigns) address root causes, quick wins (e.g., pilots or lightweight interventions) enable agile testing and momentum. Holding both perspectives ensures the work remains both strategic and actionable.

Impact

Visible indicators:

  • 4 half-day workshops conducted
  • 15+ one-on-one stakeholder deep-dive engagement sessions
  • 6 focus areas identified
  • 2 current- and ideal-state service journeys developed
  • 1 final presentation delivered to senior management
  • Informed service improvements targeted to reach ~1.2 million enrolled citizens

Non-visible indicators:

  • Surfaced policy gaps on role clarity, resource constraints, and clinical protocol design
  • Spun off pilots to test workflow redesign concepts on the ground
  • Strengthened cross-agency alignment and shared ownership of resident engagement challenges
  • Built a foundation for strategic alignment through creating shared language, artifacts, and priorities
