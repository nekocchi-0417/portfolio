Elevating the Usability of Hotel Management Application

A client project to redesign the interface of a hotel management application by understanding the users' behavioural and cognitive patterns.

UX Designer

Figma, Miro

Overview

This project centered around improving the user-friendliness of the Hotel Operation Management application. My role involved conducting usability evaluation, creating wireframes, and providing recommendations to the client as part of a tender pitch.

Problem

The client aimed to design a one-stop solution for hotel management. This spans managing cleaning, service delivery, and complaints and overseeing overall operations.

However, initial user feedback reflected that the current interface is cluttered and challenging to navigate.

Solution

The core functions of the app were already laid out. Our value add was to redesign the application taking into account the users' behavioural and cognitive patterns.

In the end, we delivered an improved interface with a simplified layout and intuitive navigation to enhance usability and overall efficiency.

Understanding the Users

Based on secondary research, I crafted user personas representing the hotel managers. These personas highlighted their goals, motivations, and challenges, allowing us to empathise with their needs and tailor design solutions accordingly.

Note: Due to time constraints and lack of access to real-world users, we went creative with our user research. We mined blog posts detailing a day in the life of a hotel manager, analysed job descriptions, and pestered our clients for second-hand insights instead.

Wireframes

The wireframing process involved translating the user needs and design considerations into a revamped interface. I created low-fidelity wireframes to visualize the new layout.

Feature 1: Navigation Menu

The first feature we redesigned was the horizontal navigation menu at the top. The redesigned filter helps to simplify task prioritisation. The added feature of a topline summary took into account the prioritisation metrics of hotel managers.

Feature 2: Job Order Cards

The second feature we redesigned was the job order cards. We rejigged the visual layout to reflect common reading patterns. The new information architecture introduces other important information (e.g., time limit, urgency) in an easy-to-digest manner.

Other screens

Apart from the wireframes above, I re-designed ~12 other screens and provided 25 recommendations in total. These are excluded from this case study in adherence to the Non-Disclosure Agreement, due to the specificity of the features.

Most of them revolved around improving usability through:

  • Introducing functionalities and shortcuts to streamline the interaction flow
  • Improving information architecture
  • Rethinking the interaction dynamic between supervisors and runners in terms of how they delegate and execute job orders
  • Early error detection and easy error diagnosis
  • Designing for Accessibility

Because the managers and runners in the hospitality sector often come from diverse backgrounds (e.g., race, language, age), we also incorporated the following recommendations:

  • Text size adjustors and contrasting colours — to ensure legibility and ease of reading for seniors, who tend to be the majority of the runner population.
  • Language selectors — to cater to non-English-speaking populations.

The actual pitch and client feedback

The pitch went well and the Head of Product provided positive feedback. He feedbacked that his product team had grappled with usability issues for weeks, struggling to arrive at effective redesigns. Our recommendations aligned with their challenges and offered concrete, actionable references that had strong potential to move their design process forward.

Key learnings

As my first independent client project, this was an extremely valuable learning experience. I took away 3 important lessons:

  1. Be thick-skinned. I learnt that the old adage "ask and you shall receive" holds true — a couple of generous colleagues kindly agreed to be part of the usability testing and brainstorming process. The quick sessions yielded a wealth of insights that shaped the final design and strengthened the pitch.
  2. See the parallels. Be it digging into the company's best practice repositories, learning from colleagues' past projects, or drawing inspiration from apps across sectors (e.g., e-commerce, productivity), I learnt to be resourceful. This experience captivated me with UX's cross-domain relevance.
  3. The art of communication. In my final pitch, I used positive framings — recasting design issues as "opportunities". Instead of only emphasising issues, I also highlighted the existing interface's strengths to help build rapport. This helped the client to be more receptive to feedback.
