Policy Concept Testing — Scenario-based debate

Simulating scenarios for different policy options to better understand benefits and trade-offs.

Main officer scoping and facilitating the workshop

2 officers, 2 team leads

2 weeks

Problem

As Singapore shifts towards preventive care, multiple public healthcare organisations have begun piloting digital interventions aimed at influencing lifestyle behaviours, leading to a proliferation of behavioural nudging initiatives in the digital health space.

However, the ecosystem is fragmented, with growing concerns over nudge fatigue on the citizen's front and resource duplication on the system's front.

Additionally, agencies have divergent stakes in different governance models (e.g., centralised vs decentralised), resulting in an impasse around system coordination.

Solution

To break the deadlock, we distilled the governance choices into two archetypal policy options representing opposite ends of the centralisation–decentralisation spectrum. We then convened stakeholders from across policy, implementation, and research to debate the merits and risks of both options.

This structure encouraged perspective-taking and surfaced previously unspoken assumptions.

The discussion culminated in a policy paper on recommendations for coordinating digital health interventions across public healthcare, which was presented to senior management.

Workshop Approach

Recruitment of Participants

Participants were recruited from diverse functions including data analytics, tech, programme, and innovation teams. They represented various public sector organisations with differing stakes in the policy outcome.

This diversity of perspectives was critical to stress-testing the governance options across operational, strategic, and systemic dimensions.

Scenario-based Debate

To facilitate structured deliberation, we adopted a scenario-based debate format centered on two contrasting governance models:

  • Centralised coordination – clear delineation of roles and responsibilities, with defined mandates for each organisation on the types of digital health interventions they are responsible for delivering.
  • Decentralised autonomy – allowing each organisation to innovate and run their own intervention.

Participants were assigned to either one of the two models. Each group was tasked to advocate for their assigned policy option and to imagine what their assigned option looks like if implemented in the future.

Participants began with individually writing down perceived benefits and risks on post-its, which were then clustered and discussed collectively. The individual activity was introduced intentionally to reduce groupthink and to encourage different perspectives before the collective discussion.

Midway through the session, participants switched roles to argue for the opposing policy option.

This role reversal was key in prompting participants to critically examine trade-offs, confront assumptions, and uncover blind spots in their original positions. It compelled organisations with strong leanings toward a particular stance to consider the rationale, constraints, and potential value of the alternative, fostering more empathetic, system-level thinking.

By the end of the session, partners came to a consensus that both models carried inherent trade-offs, and that success would depend not on choosing one over the other, but on a clear understanding of the conditions under which each model might succeed or fail.

Key Findings

Finding 1

Decentralisation enables innovation but creates friction for system-level coordination.

Organisations that typically favoured decentralisation came to appreciate the risks of resource duplication on a system level, the challenges of scaling initiatives due to divergent developments, and the difficulty of decommissioning initiatives due to sunk cost and institutional inertia.

Success would be dependent on having a clear consolidation process, such as clear evaluation processes to determine scaling/decommissioning of initiatives.

Finding 2

Centralisation offers clarity but risks oversimplifying complexity.

Those inclined toward centralisation acknowledged that while a unified model offers coherence and consistency, it may lack the agility to address local needs and manage transitional/liminal cases.

Success would be dependent on strong cross-agency collaboration to leverage complementary capabilities, refine shared efforts, and address use cases in the grey area. This must be supported by key enablers like data sharing, system integration, and clear governance.

Finding 3

Governance models must be anchored in operational context and tested.

There was also a shared recognition that neither model could be fully evaluated in theory alone. To move forward, these governance options needed to be piloted in the context of on-the-ground programmes to validate assumptions and assess feasibility.

Informing policy and system coordination

The insights informed a policy paper that outlined the key governance options and their respective trade-offs. By articulating the underlying considerations, the paper made the case for piloting one of the models. This recommendation was endorsed by senior management and will be followed by a service journey mapping session, where agencies will collaboratively map their on-the-ground programmes, overlapping touchpoints, and nudges — laying the groundwork for stronger coordination at a systems level by informing downstream tech implementation.
