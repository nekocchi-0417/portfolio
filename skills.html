Sidetrack: What is Service Design?

"If you asked 10 people what service design is, you would end up with 11 different answers—at least."

The simple answer is....

"When you have two coffee shops right next to each other, and each sells the exact same coffee at the exact same price, service design is what makes you walk into one and not the other."
– This is Service Design Thinking (Stickdorn, 2011)

The more complex answer:

Service design is about designing systems of interaction, incentives, and touchpoints across the entire service journey. It considers not just what a user sees or does at the front stage, but also the underlying structures, processes, and stakeholder coordination that enable that experience to happen seamlessly. This means that service design projects may not always produce tangible designs like interface screens. Instead, they often result in systems maps, journey blueprints, redefined roles, or implementation strategies — tools that reshape how services function behind the scenes.

In my portfolio, you'll see how this plays out: from co-creating ideal service journeys with stakeholders, mapping pain points across touchpoints, to simulating system interventions. The focus isn't just on what users see, but on designing the conditions that make meaningful experiences possible.

How does Service design intersect with Behavioural Insights?

In my experience at MOH, I naturally found myself drawing from both toolkits to design better services — service design to navigate the complexity of the public healthcare system, and behavioural insights to address the nuances of individual behaviour change.

I found that there is great potential in marrying the two as...

  • The field of behavioural insights provide a behaviourally anchored framework (e.g., COM-B) to understand why users struggle—not just where.
  • Service design methodologies lay out a structured mechanism (e.g., journey mapping) to embed behavioural interventions in real-world service experiences, where there are multiple other touchpoints and interactions in the journey.
  • The result is a shared decision-making tool that allows multidisciplinary teams to see when, why, and how to intervene.
